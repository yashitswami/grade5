Teacher's Lesson Plan Overview

Topic: Matter — What Is It? What Are Its States?

Grade: 5  |  Duration: 60 minutes  |  Approach: Experience First, Name Later

PHASEACTIVITYTIMEWHAT THE TEACHER DOES
🪄 HOOK The Mystery Rock & Cloud
Show ice, water, steam (or a melting ice cube). Ask: "Are all three the same thing?"		 5–7 min Create cognitive dissonance. Accept all guesses. Write them on the board. Do NOT explain yet.
🧪 EXPLORE Station Experiments
Groups test: sugar in water, blowing up a balloon, squeezing a sealed syringe, comparing ice/water/air.		 15–18 min Circulate. Ask "What do you notice? Does it change shape? Can you compress it?" Never give answers. Give a prediction sheet before starting.
💬 DISCUSS Group Share-Out
"What did your group observe? Who got a different result?"		 10–12 min Use Socratic questions. Write student observations on the board in THEIR words. Connect groups' findings.
📖 CONCEPT Name the Discovery
Introduce: Matter, Solid, Liquid, Gas, Particles. Connect each to what they observed.		 10 min Draw particle diagrams LIVE on board. Use their words first, then scientific terms. Max 3 new vocab words.
✅ APPLY New Scenario + Exit Ticket
"What state is perfume when you spray it? What about honey?"		 10–12 min Let students debate. Then Exit Ticket: 3 sentences. Collect and read before next class.
🎯 Learning Goals (by end of class)
Students can: (1) name the 3 states of matter from observation, (2) describe how particles are arranged differently in each state, (3) give one real-life example of each state, (4) ask one new question about matter.

Phase 1 — The Hook

🪄 The Wonder Starter (5–7 minutes)

Walk in with an ice cube on a plate. Hold it up and ask three questions — then wait:

  • "What is this made of?" (water)
  • "What is the steam above a boiling kettle made of?" (water)
  • "If both are water... why do they look and behave so differently?"

Write all student guesses on the board. Don't correct any. Then say: "Let's find out."

Why this works

The brain is now hungry. You've created a gap between what they think they know and what they don't know. This is called a curiosity gap — and it's the most powerful motivator for learning. The next 45 minutes will feel like solving a mystery, not sitting in a lecture.

Ice, liquid water, and steam — all made of the same substance, behaving completely differently.

2

Phase 2 — Explore (Station Experiments)

Set up 3 stations for groups of 3–4 students

Give each group a Prediction Sheet first: "What do you think will happen? Write your guess before trying!"

🧂 Station A — Sugar Disappears (Liquid)

Materials: A glass of water, a spoon of sugar

1
Predict: Where will the sugar go?
2
Stir the sugar into the water. Watch carefully.
3
Taste the water. What do you notice?
4
Discuss: Did the sugar disappear? Or is it still there?
Teacher Prompt
"If matter cannot be destroyed, where is the sugar now?" — Let students debate. Don't confirm.
🎈 Station B — Invisible Air (Gas)

Materials: A balloon, a sealed plastic syringe (with no needle)

1
Blow up the balloon. Feel its weight — does it weigh more now?
2
Push the syringe plunger down with the tip sealed. What do you feel?
3
Let go. What happens?
4
Discuss: If you can't see air, is it real? Does it take up space?
Teacher Prompt
"Can you compress the water in the same way?" — (No, you cannot.) "Why not?"
🧊 Station C — Ice vs Water vs Air (Compare All 3)

Materials: Ice cube, glass of water, empty sealed bottle

1
Put the ice in a bowl. Does it keep its shape or take the shape of the bowl?
2
Pour water into different containers. What shape does it take?
3
Try to squeeze the sealed bottle slightly. What happens to the air inside?
4
Make a table: Which one keeps its shape? Which fills its container? Which can be squeezed?

Phase 3 → Concept: The Three States

From your experiments, you discovered this yourself:

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. Everything you can touch, smell, or see is matter. Even air!

Matter exists in three main states — and you just explored all three:

Particle arrangement in solids, liquids, and gases — this is WHY they behave differently.

Property Solid 🧊 Liquid 💧 Gas 💨
Fixed Shape? ✅ Yes ❌ No ❌ No
Fixed Volume? ✅ Yes ✅ Yes ❌ No
Can be compressed? ❌ No ❌ No ✅ Yes
Particles Tightly packed Loosely packed Very far apart

The Particle Idea — Matter Up Close

Everything is made of tiny particles — too small to see.

Imagine a crowd at a cricket stadium. When the stadium is full, everyone is packed tight — they can only wiggle in place. That's a solid. After the match, people start leaving — now they can move around each other. That's a liquid. Outside the stadium, people scatter everywhere — they can go anywhere. That's a gas.

🤔 Think About This

When you open a perfume bottle across the room, you can smell it without touching it. How did the particles of perfume reach your nose? What state of matter does this tell you perfume particles become?

The stadium analogy — particles in solids, liquids, and gases behave like crowds in different situations.

5

Matter Can Change Its State

Heat and cold can change matter from one state to another — but it's still the same matter!

This is key: the amount of matter doesn't change, only its state does. Ice → water → steam is still all water.

The water cycle in your kitchen — melting, evaporation, condensation, and freezing.

🌍 Real World Connection
The water cycle on Earth is matter changing states constantly! Rain (liquid) → puddles evaporate (gas) → clouds form (condensation) → rain again. The same water has been cycling for millions of years.
